About

Curious about what shapes us

I am an undergraduate student in financial engineering, still learning how to ask good questions. I am drawn to the puzzle of invisible forces that quietly shape the lives people end up living — forces that are sometimes centuries old and sometimes painfully modern. The kinship structures of Qing China, the dowry taxes of Renaissance Florence, the zodiac beliefs of rural China — these are ancient institutions whose echoes persist. A single unexpected court ruling, a rise in wet-bulb temperature, or the genes we inherit — these are modern shocks whose consequences I am only beginning to understand.

A recurring question across my work is whether the effects we observe are truly stable or merely appear so. Are gender peer effects as equal as they seem, or do they mask deeper asymmetries? Do the beliefs that move commodity prices come from genuine uncertainty or from disagreement among market participants? Does the biology of lactose tolerance become a blessing or a curse in a world where milk is still dangerous? In each case I try to find a clean natural experiment and let the data speak, whether the setting is a Qing dynasty household, a Florentine tax archive, a modern Chinese classroom, or a global commodity market.

I have been very fortunate to work with wonderful collaborators on datasets from Taiwan, Bolivia, Ecuador, the UK, and historical China and Russia. Several projects examine how peers shape us: whether early puberty spills over to classmates, whether body weight norms transmit through school networks, and whether talent classes generate positive externalities for peers. Others investigate the intersection of genes and environment: how bullying interacts with genetic predispositions to affect patience and mental health, and how birth order and disability were intertwined in premodern China. I still have far more to learn than I have learned, and I am always grateful for feedback, criticism, or the chance to collaborate. Outside of work I play Go (Weiqi), grateful to Lin Yi-Ting 3p for her patience and guidance.

Hand-Grinding Tips for Beginners
Grinder: A hand grinder with conical steel burrs (e.g., 1Zpresso, Timemore) is far superior to any blade grinder. Consistency of grind is the single most important factor.
Grind size: Finer = more extraction (more bitterness), coarser = less extraction (more sourness). Adjust by half a turn at a time.
Water: Use filtered water, not distilled. TDS around 150 ppm is ideal. Temperature matters: lighter roasts need lower temps (90-92 C), darker roasts need higher (93-95 C).
Ratio: Start with 1:15 (1g coffee per 15g water) and adjust to taste. Weigh everything.
Bloom: Always pour twice the coffee weight in water first and wait 30 seconds.
Freshness: Beans are best 7-21 days after roasting. Grind immediately before brewing.

LeBron James
King James
Lakers #6

I am, without apology, a LeBron James fan. Not because he is the greatest basketball player of all time — though I believe he is — but because of what his story represents.

LeBron was born to a single sixteen-year-old mother in Akron, Ohio. He grew up moving from apartment to apartment, sometimes missing months of school at a time. The odds of a kid from that background becoming anything — let alone one of the most dominant athletes in history — were vanishingly small. And yet he did it, not through luck alone, but through a discipline and work ethic that has never wavered over more than two decades in the NBA.

What moves me most is not the championships or the records, but the consistency. Year after year, season after season, LeBron has shown up. He has taken care of his body with a scientific rigor that borders on obsession. He has adapted his game as he aged, from the explosive athleticism of his youth to the cerebral playmaking of his later years. He has spoken out on social issues when it would have been easier to stay silent. He built a school in his hometown.

And then there is the humor. For all his intensity, LeBron has never lost his sense of playfulness on the court — the exaggerated celebrations, the impromptu dance moves, the way he grins after a ridiculous pass. I have learned from him that even in the most high-pressure moments, it is okay to laugh, to enjoy yourself, to not take everything so seriously. I try to bring that spirit into my own work: when the data won't cooperate or the code keeps breaking, sometimes the best thing to do is step back and smile. LeBron shows that greatness and joy are not opposites — they are companions.

As a student who often feels overwhelmed by how much there is to learn, I find LeBron's example deeply encouraging. He shows that greatness is not a single moment of brilliance but a lifetime of showing up, doing the work, and refusing to settle. I will never be the LeBron James of economics — but I can try to show up every day and give my best effort. That is the lesson I take from him.
